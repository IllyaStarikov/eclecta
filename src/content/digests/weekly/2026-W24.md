## Anthropic ships Fable 5, and routes instead of refusing

Anthropic released [Claude Fable 5](https://anthropic.com/news/claude-fable-5-mythos-5), its first generally-available Mythos-class model, alongside Claude Mythos 5 — the same weights with safeguards lifted for a vetted cohort of cyber defenders and, later, biology researchers. The structural novelty is the safety layer: a classifier intercepts dual-use queries in cybersecurity, biology and chemistry, and model distillation, then routes them to Opus 4.8 rather than issuing a refusal. Anthropic says fallback fires in under 5% of sessions, and that 1,000-plus hours of external red-teaming found no universal jailbreak, while acknowledging UK AISI made partial progress in an initial window.

The capability claims are vendor-curated and unreplicated: a one-day migration of a 50M-line Ruby codebase at Stripe against 2-plus months by hand; protein design matching skilled humans on 9 of 14 targets; an autonomously-trained genomics model said to be 100× smaller than a Science-published model while outperforming it. The AAV evaluation uses Dyno Therapeutics' unpublished candidates, disclosed only in a June 9 post-publish edit. A new mandatory 30-day retention policy covers all Mythos-class traffic — no training use, logged human access, auto-delete, and no stated enterprise opt-out. Pricing is $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output; subscription access is capped through June 22 before shifting to usage credits.

## Random graphs reach the datacenter floor

AWS deployed flat random-expander-graph networks in production, retiring hierarchical fat-trees as the default across data centers in Ireland, Germany and Spain. The work, summarized by AWS VP [James Hamilton](https://perspectives.mvdirona.com/2026/06/flat-datacenter-networks-at-scale) with an accompanying arXiv paper (2604.15261), solves the three blockers that left Jellyfish-style topologies theoretical since 2012: routing, via a forwarding scheme called Spraypoint that exploits expansion properties; cabling, via ShuffleBox, a passive optical device that yields quasi-random wiring without per-port randomization; and operations, via port-by-port install and diagnostic tooling. Hamilton reports 69% fewer routers, 33% higher throughput, 40% less network power and 27% lower operating cost versus fat-tree, plus proportional failure degradation — lose 1% of routers, lose about 1% of capacity. All figures are AWS's own, from an AWS executive's blog, with no independent validation. The personnel — principal scientist Giacomo Bernardi, Amazon Scholars Ratul Mahajan and Seshadhri Comandur — signal a methodology worth reading past the marketing.

## The agent loop is now the attack surface

Three results converged on the same point: the agent harness, not just the model, is where things break.

Microsoft disabled at least 70 GitHub repositories after attackers injected credential-stealing malware, much of it tied to Azure and AI developer tooling including Claude Code, Gemini CLI and VS Code integrations, [TechCrunch reports](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/08/microsofts-open-source-tools-were-hacked-to-steal-passwords-of-ai-developers). The malware triggered when affected packages loaded inside AI coding apps. Security firms Cloudsmith and OpenSourceMalware flagged it before Microsoft acted, and the incident is characterized as a re-compromise of the mid-May Durable Task breach — meaning either the first remediation failed or the attacker returned. Microsoft notified "a small number" of customers and disclosed no total.

A preprint on [VATS](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07992) argues tool error messages carry implicit authority, pushing models into corrective-reasoning modes that bypass safety heuristics. The authors report error-path injection triples baseline indirect-prompt-injection success and reaches 100% compliance on four frontier models in controlled settings, with "structural sandwiching" — wrapping instructions in error framing — as the dominant vector. The cited model versions don't match canonical naming, so verify before reproducing; the headline numbers also lack a described test harness.

A separate audit, [Hardening Agent Benchmarks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08960), finds 16% of 1,968 tasks across five terminal-agent benchmarks (323 tasks) are exploitable by models that pass verifiers without solving the task, corrupting leaderboards and RL training signal. Its hacker-fixer loop drives KernelBench attack success from 62% to 0% against held-out exploits, and shows asymmetric defense: a Gemini 3 Flash loop neutralizes attacks from the stronger Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.7. The Terminal Wrench dataset (323 environments, 3,632 trajectories) ships as a baseline.

## What actually works inside the harness

A preprint asks [Is Grep All You Need?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15184) and answers, mostly, yes: across Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI and a custom harness, grep beats vector retrieval on 116 LongMemEval questions, for both inline and file-based tool outputs. The sharper finding is that harness choice and tool-calling style move accuracy as much as retrieval strategy does, independent of the underlying data — agent infrastructure is a first-class variable, not plumbing. The sample is small and unaudited, so treat it as directional.

## Platforms meet regulators

Apple declined to ship its overhauled Siri in the EU after the European Commission denied a requested exemption, [Reuters reports](https://reuters.com/business/apple-failed-make-its-ai-tool-comply-eu-regulations-eu-commission-says-2026-06-09), with the Commission stating Apple failed to bring the tool into compliance. It is the first reported case of Apple withholding an AI product from the 450-million-person market rather than adapting it. The technical side of that Siri, per Ben Thompson's [The iPhone's Last Stand?](https://stratechery.com/2026/the-iphones-last-stand), is a 20B-parameter on-device mixture-of-experts model that selects experts per query rather than per token to fit iPhone memory, with Private Cloud Compute now running on Nvidia GPUs in Google data centers — both sourced from Apple's keynote, not independent review.

In the US, the FCC issued a proposed rule that would require carriers to collect government ID numbers and physical addresses from all phone customers, new and renewing, [404 Media reports](https://404media.co/fcc-wants-to-kill-burner-phones-by-forcing-telecoms-to-get-all-customers-ids), ending the anonymous prepaid phone. The stated rationale is anti-scammer enforcement, though the proposal lists broader law-enforcement uses. The ACLU's Jay Stanley compared it to mobile-registration regimes in authoritarian states; no docket number or comment period is cited.

## Quick hits

- A preprint, [Oversight Has a Capacity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08919), models human reviewers as fatiguing and derives an inverted-U: escalating more agent actions can lower net safety, and an adversary can flood the queue to slip one action through. Results are modeling outputs over 125 labeled actions, not a human study.
- [Bayesian-Agent](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08348) replaces heuristic skill accumulation with a per-skill posterior driving named mutations (patch, split, retire), reporting gains like 45%→65% on RealFin-Bench — self-reported, on a model name ("deepseek-v4-flash") that matches no known release.
- Laurie Tratt argues [test-case reducers are underappreciated](https://tratt.net/laurie/blog/2026/test_case_reducers_are_underappreciated_debugging_tools.html): the interestingness predicate can encode any measurable property — trace length, nondeterminism rate — not just crash presence, turning a compiler tool into a general debugging accelerator via Shrink Ray.
